13F and Institutional Holdings
The data foundation for cross-holding analysis

The first five lessons covered company-disclosed information — 10-K, 10-Q, 8-K, and Earnings Calls are all from the company's perspective. This lesson turns to how third parties view the company — who is buying, who is selling, who is heavily positioned, who is withdrawing. This is the core data source for the IHS Markit report (P4 cross-holding analysis).

13F is the SEC-mandated form on which large institutional investors disclose their holdings; the full name is Schedule 13F. It is public, free, and updated quarterly, which is why it has become the de facto standard for cross-holding analysis.

Almost all of P2-P7 of the IHS Markit reference report is built on 13F data. Our system also uses 13F — we just switched the data source from IHS to SEC EDGAR.

What 13F Is

Treat 13F as a "quarterly report card" for institutional investors — but there are some hard rules to know:

RuleSpecific RequirementStrategic Implication
Legal basisSecurities Exchange Act of 1934, Section 13(f)Statutory disclosure; failure to file is illegal
Filing thresholdInstitutional investors with > $100M in assets under managementCovers essentially all large mutual funds, hedge funds, pensions, and ETF issuers
Filing frequencyWithin 45 days of quarter-endQ1 deadline 5/15, Q2 deadline 8/14, Q3 deadline 11/14, Q4 deadline 2/14
Number of filersApproximately 5,000-6,000 institutionsBasically covers the US "smart money"
13F reflects a quarter-end snapshot — not intra-quarter trading. A stock may be bought in March and sold in April, and 13F will show no sign of it. The "Turnover" column in the IHS Markit report is an "approximate rebalancing" inferred from two adjacent 13F periods, not actual trade flow.

What 13F Discloses — and What It Does Not

Understanding the boundaries of 13F is more important than knowing what it discloses — because these boundaries directly determine what questions it can answer and which ones it cannot.

What is disclosed (what is in 13F)

  • For each position: name, CUSIP, share count, market value
  • The market value field is called value_x1000, with units in thousands of US dollars (not dollars, not millions of dollars)
  • Covers long positions in all US-registered securities

What is not disclosed (13F blind spots)

Cannot seeWhy notImpact on analysis
Short positions13F only reports longsHedge fund short bets are completely missing — and that may be the strongest signal
Options / derivatives13F excludes derivativesMany hedge funds express views through options; 13F cannot see those
Non-US holdings13F only covers US stocksGlobal institutions' non-US positions are completely invisible
Non-listed securitiesPrivate debt and unlisted companies are out of scopePE funds and private credit funds' activity is invisible
Intra-quarter rebalancingOnly quarter-end snapshots are shownDaily, weekly, and monthly trades are all averaged into "quarter-end holdings"
Filers with < $100M AUMBelow threshold, no filing requiredSmall institutions and retail are not covered
Remember this: 13F shows you the "photo at quarter-end," not the "movie of the quarter." If you want the movie, you need finer-grained data (e.g. Bloomberg trade flow, WhaleWisdom premium).

Unit Conversion Pitfalls (a High-Frequency Bug Zone)

The most error-prone part of 13F data processing is unit conversion. The table below shows real bug patterns:

Raw SEC XML: <value>12345</value>  →  meaning: $12,345,000  →  $12.345M
edgartools returns: field name value_x1000 → value 12345 → meaning: 12345 x $1,000

Common mistakes:
  Error A: Display value_x1000 directly as "thousands of dollars" → number looks like 12,345
          → actually $12.345M — off by 1,000x
  Error B: Take 12345 and divide by 1,000 to display as 12.345 — off by 1,000,000x
  Error C: Don't distinguish market-value magnitudes when aggregating
          → Vanguard holds $1.2B in CVNA (value_x1000 = 1,200,000)
          → BlackRock holds $3M in UXIN (value_x1000 = 3,000)
          → If you SUM directly, UXIN's moves are drowned out by CVNA (400x difference)

Validation method:
  1. Get Vanguard's raw CVNA 13F XML
  2. Manually read the <value> field = X
  3. Compare with the parsed value_x1000 from edgartools
  4. Cross-check with CVNA's then-current price x share count
     (should roughly match, within ±5%)

⚠️ How our system handles this

Throughout the data flow the value_x1000 field stays in SEC's raw unit (thousands of dollars); only at the dashboard display layer do we divide by 1,000 to show millions ($M). All internal calculations (aggregations, QoQ changes, Top Buyers/Sellers rankings) are based on a uniform "$M" unit, to avoid mixing units at different stages of the data flow.

The Right Way to Compare 13F Period-over-Period

The core operation of cross-holding analysis is "comparing two adjacent 13F periods." This logic must be implemented correctly, or the Top Buyers/Sellers ranking will be completely distorted.

Boundary conditions (the 4 cases that must be handled)

  1. New position: curr_value > 0 and prev_value == 0 — institution appears for the first time
  2. Full liquidation: curr_value == 0 and prev_value > 0 — institution fully exits
  3. Increase: curr_value > prev_value — buying more
  4. Decrease: curr_value < prev_value — trimming
  5. No change: curr_value == prev_value — no movement (rare; only when shares and price both stay flat)
curr = holdings WHERE report_period = '2026-03-31'
prev = holdings WHERE report_period = '2025-12-31'
merged = curr FULL OUTER JOIN prev ON (institution_cik, ticker)

for each row:
    if curr_value > 0 and prev_value == 0:  → Initiation
    if curr_value == 0 and prev_value > 0:  → Liquidation
    if curr_value > prev_value:              → Increase
    if curr_value < prev_value:              → Decrease
    if curr_value == prev_value:             → No change

Notes:
  - When prev_value = 0, do not compute delta_pct (division by zero)
  - After FULL OUTER JOIN, institution_name may be missing on one side; backfill it
  - Period-over-period comparison must be between adjacent periods (no skipping), or the changes will be artificially inflated

For each stock, the change = active rebalancing + price movement

If an institution holds 10,000 shares of CVNA, and the current 13F still shows 10,000 shares, but the price rose from $100 to $150:

  • Share count unchanged = the institution did not actively trade
  • But market value rose from $1,000,000 to $1,500,000 = purely a result of price appreciation

This "passive market value appreciation" can be misclassified as "adding to a position," misleading the Top Buyers ranking. The correct approach is to look at share change (shares_change), not market value change (value_change).

Core principle: when comparing 13F period-over-period, look at shares, not market value, to judge active rebalancing. Market value changes are contaminated by price moves; only share changes are true "buys/sells."

13F can answer "who is buying and selling," but it cannot answer "why" — the latter requires management commentary (13F does not disclose the rationale for trades). This is why 13F changes must be cross-validated with 8-Ks and Earnings Calls.

Quiz

Q1: The value_x1000 field in a 13F report has a value of 12345. How many US dollars is that?

Click to reveal answer

12,345,000 US dollars (i.e. $12.345M)

The semantics of value_x1000 is "in thousands of US dollars." So a value of 12345 = 12345 x $1,000 = $12,345,000 ≈ $12.3M. A common mistake is to display it directly as "$12,345" or to divide by 1,000 to show "$12.345," each off by a factor of 1,000 or 1,000,000 respectively.

Q2: An institution's share count in CVNA went from 1 million to 1 million shares this quarter, and market value went from $100M to $150M. Does this count as "adding to the position"?

Click to reveal answer

No, it is passive market value appreciation. Unchanged shares = no active trading. The market value change came entirely from CVNA's price moving from $100 to $150. In the Top Buyers ranking, you should sort by share change (shares delta), not market value change — otherwise "hold and let the price rise" gets misclassified as "actively adding."

Q3: Can 13F tell us about "hedge funds' short positions in CVNA"?

Click to reveal answer

No. 13F only discloses long positions, not shorts or options. To see short positions you need a separate data source (e.g. S3 Partners, Markit Short Interest, FINRA's bi-monthly short interest report). This is why the IHS Markit report explicitly flags "data limitations" to remind readers of 13F's blind spots.
