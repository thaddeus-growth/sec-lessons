8-K Events Deep Dive
What is worth "escalating upward"

Lesson 0001 explained what an 8-K is. This lesson covers how to judge the importance of an 8-K. This is the part of the competitor monitoring system that demands the most human judgment, and it is also where you, as the head of strategic investment, add the most value — the system makes sure nothing is missed, and you make sure nothing is misinterpreted.

Case Study: Carvana's Three Recent 8-Ks

Case 1: 2026-05-06 8-K — Items 5.02, 5.03, 5.07

Items: 5.02 (Officer/Director changes) + 5.03 (Charter amendments) + 5.07 (Shareholder vote results)

🔴 Escalate upward. Three Items appeared together, and 5.02 is an officer change — the single most important category of 8-K items. Combined with 5.03 (charter amendments), the company's governance structure may be shifting.

Follow-up actions: Check who changed (CEO? CFO? COO?), check Form 4 filings for large insider sales (would make this even more serious), and check whether last quarter's results were unusual (was the officer pushed out due to performance issues?).

Case 2: 2026-04-29 8-K — Items 2.02, 9.01

Items: 2.02 (Results of operations) + 9.01 (Exhibits/financial statements)

🟡 Routine watch. This is the most common type of 8-K — the quarterly earnings release. Item 2.02 by itself is not important (the content is essentially a notice: "we filed our earnings"). What matters is the press release issued alongside it, where the actual numbers appear.

Standard action: Compare the press release numbers with the 10-Q and with the year-ago period. Only escalate when the numbers are "surprising" (significantly above or below expectations).

Case 3: 2026-02-18 8-K — Items 2.02, 9.01

Identical to Case 2 — this was the annual earnings release. Same pattern.

🟡 Routine watch. Same as the quarterly earnings release. The annual version adds one thing: check the Guidance (next-year outlook). What does management expect next year? Is this expectation better or worse than the one they gave at the same time last year?

The Most Common Judgment Errors

Error 1: "Item 2.02 just dropped — report it now!"
Wrong. 2.02 comes with every quarterly report; by itself it is not important. What matters are the numbers in the press release. Do not confuse "there are events" with "there are important events."
Error 2: "Form 144 — an insider is reducing — that is bearish!"
Not necessarily. Form 144 filings are frequently insiders executing under a 10b5-1 plan (a pre-arranged trading program) tied to normal financial planning, not a negative signal. You have to distinguish "planned diversification selling" from "panic-driven, one-off selling."
Error 3: "I am the only one who received this 8-K — nobody else knows — I have an information edge."
This is SEC public data. Every hedge fund and your competitors received it at the same time. Your edge is not being early; it is interpreting it quickly and accurately.
The system delivers "no miss." You deliver "no false alarm." The core of 8-K judgment is knowing what is a real signal versus what is simply a required disclosure.

Quiz

Q1: The CFO departs (Item 5.02) versus a regular VP departs (also Item 5.02). Should these be handled the same way?

Click to reveal answer

No, they should not. A CFO departure must be escalated immediately and investigated for accompanying financial issues; a VP departure can be logged and monitored. The CFO holds the company's most sensitive financial information. If the timing is close to an earnings release or auditor change, it is a danger sign. A VP departure can be a routine career move, unless the VP runs a core business line as a Senior Vice President.

Q2: A competitor filed two 8-Ks on the same day (one Item 5.02 officer departure, one Item 4.01 auditor change). What is your judgment?

Click to reveal answer

This is a major red flag — escalate immediately. An auditor change plus an officer departure occurring at the same time usually means a serious financial conflict inside the company. The auditor may have found something they were unwilling to sign off on, and the officer (most often the CFO) may have been forced out as a result. This pattern has appeared repeatedly in financial fraud cases. Even if it turns out to be nothing, you must be on high alert right now — better to misjudge than to miss it.

Q3: Under what circumstances does an Item 2.02 (earnings release) become worth "escalating upward"?

Click to reveal answer

When the numbers clearly deviate from expectations. Specifically: revenue or net income deviates from market consensus by more than 20%; or management proactively issues a guidance cut for the next quarter. Otherwise, 2.02 is routine and should be handled through the normal flow.
