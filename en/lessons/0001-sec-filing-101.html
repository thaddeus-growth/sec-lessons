SEC Filing 101
Understanding the US public-company disclosure system through three forms

Every US-listed public company is required to periodically file documents with the SEC (US Securities and Exchange Commission), and to publish those filings worldwide through the EDGAR system. This disclosure framework is one of the most mature and transparent capital-market infrastructures in the world.

For a strategic investor, SEC filings are free, mandatory, structured competitor intelligence — more accurate than the news, more objective than third-party research, and more reliable than informal打听. And anyone can access them; no payment, no registration required.

Core idea: SEC filings are not a "compliance burden"—they are the strategic intelligence your competitors are forced to publish on a regular basis.

Three Core Forms

The SEC has hundreds of form types, but for competitor monitoring you only need to track three:

Carvana's fiscal year matches the calendar year (ending Dec 31), so its 10-K comes out in February and its 10-Qs in April, July, and October.

10-K: The Company's Annual "Physical Exam"

The 10-K is the single most important document a US-listed company produces. It is a legal document, and all of its contents carry legal liability (misstatements can trigger SEC enforcement actions and shareholder lawsuits).

The structure of a 10-K is fixed. It contains the following main sections:

SectionMeaningCompetitor-monitoring focus
Item 1. BusinessBusiness descriptionBusiness model, revenue sources, the company's own description of the competitive landscape
Item 1A. Risk FactorsRisk factors⚠️ Risks the company itself acknowledges—the most valuable section
Item 7. MD&AManagement's Discussion and AnalysisHow management explains results, views trends, and looks ahead
Item 8. Financial StatementsAudited financial statementsRevenue, profit, assets, liabilities, cash flow (the most reliable data)
Item 15. ExhibitsExhibitsContracts, acquisition agreements, and other original documents
The strategic investor's reading order:
Item 1A (risks) → Item 7 (what management says) → Item 8 (what the numbers say) → Item 1 (whether the business model still holds)

10-Q: The Quarterly "Checkup Summary"

You can think of the 10-Q as a "shorter 10-K + the latest period's data." The numbers are unaudited, but the company still bears legal liability for them. Its core value:

  • Track trends: four 10-Qs stitched together let you see a company's trajectory over a year
  • Spot turning points: is revenue growth accelerating or decelerating? Is gross margin improving or deteriorating?
  • Faster than the 10-K: the 10-K requires the full annual audit; the 10-Q appears within 40 days of quarter-end

8-K: The Company's "Evening News"

The 8-K is the most special of the three—it is filed on no fixed schedule, event-driven, and the most time-sensitive. By law, the company must file an 8-K within 4 business days of any "material event."

The SEC has coded every possible "material event" as an Item number, from 1.01 to 9.01. Knowing what each Item means is a core skill in competitor monitoring (the next lesson covers this in depth).

For now, just remember the most important ones:

ItemEventWhat it means for competitor monitoring
2.02Earnings releaseKey numbers appear before the 10-Q; sometimes a pre-announcement when results diverge sharply from expectations
5.02Executive / director changesCFO departure? CEO change? Can be a signal of internal trouble
1.01Material agreementsA major contract signed? Supplier change? Strategic partnership?
2.01Material asset acquisitions / dispositionsBuying a company, selling a business—a change in strategic direction
Quick test: if you can monitor only one SEC form, pick 8-K. It covers all the "unexpected events" that 10-K and 10-Q miss.

Quiz

Q1: If you want to learn a competitor's "self-acknowledged risks," which section should you read?

Click to see the answer

10-K, Item 1A (Risk Factors)

This is the company's own list of risks; it must disclose "any risks that could materially harm the business." Because it is a legal statement, it is relatively objective. Common risk disclosures for used-car companies include: vehicle-quality risk, loan-default risk, macroeconomic impact on used-car demand, and regulatory changes.

Q2: Which form must be filed within "4 business days" of an event?

Click to see the answer

8-K

The 8-K is special in that it is "event-driven"—unlike the 10-K and 10-Q, which follow a fixed schedule. So when you see a competitor suddenly file an 8-K, open it immediately—there is almost certainly a change worth watching.

Q3: Of the 10-K and 10-Q, which has audited financial data?

Click to see the answer

10-K data is independently audited; 10-Q data is not.

Audit means an outside accounting firm (e.g., a Big Four firm) has verified the accuracy of the financial data. 10-Q data is self-reported by the company and unaudited, so in theory there is room to "adjust" (though the company still bears legal liability). When doing deep analysis, use 10-K data as the baseline to calibrate 10-Q data.
